Supporting Conversations After Brain Injury

This short, accessible microlearning page provides practical guidance for having supportive conversations with survivors. It complements the “Brain Injury Basics” module created in Articulate Rise.

Communication Skill Builder

What You Will Learn

This page helps you prepare for sensitive conversations and respond in supportive, person-centered ways.

  • Prepare yourself and the environment before starting a conversation.
  • Use clear, respectful, and accessible communication strategies.
  • Offer choice, control, and emotional support to survivors.

Before You Begin a Conversation

Take a moment to prepare. Small adjustments help prevent overwhelming and build trust.

Check the Environment

Reduce noise, bright lights, or distractions if possible. Choose a spot where the survivor feels comfortable.

Check Your Pace

Plan to speak slowly and allow pauses for thinking. Survivors may need extra processing time.

Check Your Assumptions

Avoid guessing what the person needs. Instead, ask open, non-judgmental questions to understand their preferences.

During a Conversation: DOs and DON’Ts

These tips help ensure clarity, dignity, and emotional safety.

DO

  • Check in: “Is this a good time to talk?”
  • Speak in brief, clear sentences.
  • Give time for thinking and responding.
  • Ask before offering help or advice.
  • Use reflective language: “That sounds overwhelming.”

DON’T

  • Say “You’re fine” or “Just relax.”
  • Rush through topics or interrupt.
  • Change subjects without warning.
  • Assume “looking fine” equals “feeling fine.”
  • Over-explain or talk too quickly.

Helpful Phrases You Can Use

  • “Would a quieter space feel better for you?”
  • “Take your time — I’m here.”
  • “What would be most helpful right now?”
  • “Let me know if you want to pause or take a break.”
  • “Can I repeat anything or explain it another way?”

Quick Reflection

Think about a time when you supported someone who was overwhelmed or stressed.

  1. What helped in that situation?
  2. What would you do differently next time?
Sample Reflection – Expand to view

Example: “I realized I often try to fix things too quickly. Next time, I want to pause, ask more questions, and let the person guide the conversation.”

There’s no “right answer” — the goal is to build awareness and empathy.
